Qualitative Research Study

Riding Unseen: Transit Access for Marginalized Communities

Longitudinal naturalistic observation — King County Metro & Sound Transit // Solo project — Fly-on-the-Wall

Overview

This study examines how the King County public transportation system serves — and fails — marginalized communities. Observations were conducted across three interconnected systems: King County Metro Route 917, which runs through the cities of Algona and Pacific before terminating at Auburn Station; Sound Transit Route 578, an express coach connecting Auburn Station to Seattle; and the Sound Transit Sounder commuter rail line at Auburn Station.

The communities I focused on were people whose first language is not English, elderly riders, and people with disabilities — populations that largely reflect my own local neighborhood. These riders depend on public transit as a primary mode of access, yet the system's infrastructure, information design, and service frequency are rarely designed with their needs as the starting point.

Surveys and structured interviews capture what riders say about their experience. What they miss is the compounding moment of failure — when a capacity limit, a service gap, and a transfer window collapse together on a single person. Observation captures that. This study documents those moments directly.

Research Question

How does the King County public transportation system serve marginalized communities — and where does it fail them?

Methodology

This study draws on longitudinal naturalistic observation accumulated through years of regular transit use across the King County Metro and Sound Transit systems. Rather than discrete observation sessions, findings are grounded in sustained first-person immersion in the transit environment — a form of autoethnographic field observation that captures behavioral patterns and systemic failures that short-term studies cannot replicate.

Observation was conducted across three interconnected systems: King County Metro Route 917, serving the cities of Algona and Pacific before terminating at Auburn Station; Sound Transit Route 578, connecting Auburn Station to Seattle; and the Sound Transit Sounder commuter rail line at Auburn Station.

What I was watching for consistently centered on: how riders with limited mobility, limited English proficiency, or limited system familiarity navigated each transfer point; where information gaps caused confusion or distress; and where the physical infrastructure of stops, sidewalks, and vehicles created barriers rather than access. I was not a detached observer — I am a regular rider of these routes and a member of the communities they serve. That proximity is both this study's greatest methodological asset and a limitation acknowledged in the reflection.

Key Observations

  • 01 — A wheelchair user stranded by capacity, frequency, and transfer dependency I observed a wheelchair user navigate a multi-leg trip from Auburn to Seattle and back. They traveled outbound on Sound Transit Route 578 without issue. On the return, the same route passed without stopping — the bus had reached its wheelchair capacity. On Route 578, which runs on hourly headways at certain times, a single missed bus is not a minor delay. Missing the 578 connection at Auburn Station meant missing the last viable Route 917 departure to their neighborhood. What I observed was not just frustration — it was the cumulative weight of a system that had been designed without their trip in mind at any point along it.
  • 02 — A Spanish-speaking rider unable to find the right platform At Auburn Station, I observed a rider attempting to board the Sounder commuter train northbound to Seattle. The station's directional signage is static and English-only. With no dynamic information and no multilingual wayfinding, this rider could not determine which side of the tracks they needed. The stress was visible. It was only when they approached me and asked in Spanish that they were able to confirm their platform and board without missing their train. The system had offered them nothing.
  • 03 — Route 917's coverage gap isolates elderly and mobility-impaired riders Route 917 runs along the main arterials of Algona and Pacific before connecting to Auburn Station. The route does not serve the residential streets that branch off those arterials — streets where a significant portion of the community lives. For elderly riders and people with limited mobility, the distance between their home and the nearest Route 917 stop is not a minor inconvenience. It is a functional barrier to using the system at all. The route's coverage area was designed around road infrastructure, not around where riders actually live or what their physical capacity to reach a stop actually is.

Analysis & Themes

Theme 01

Service Design Assumes a Rider Who Does Not Exist

Route frequency, vehicle capacity, and transfer schedules are built around an idealized average rider who is able-bodied, punctual, and has flexibility in their trip. The observations in this study repeatedly document what happens when that assumption meets reality: a wheelchair user left at a stop, a trip chain broken by a missed hourly bus, a neighborhood made functionally unreachable by a coverage gap. The system was not designed maliciously — but it was not designed for these riders either.

Theme 02

Information Systems Exclude by Default

Static, English-only signage is not a neutral design choice. In communities where significant portions of residents are non-native English speakers, it is an active barrier. Route 917 stops display only a route number. Auburn Station's platform signage offers no multilingual support. The assumption embedded in these systems is that riders arrive already knowing what they need to know — an assumption that fails the people who need transit most.

Theme 03

Infrastructure Investment Tracks Wealth, Not Need

The contrast between Route 917's corridor and transit infrastructure in higher-income Seattle neighborhoods is not incidental. It reflects a pattern of capital investment that follows demographics rather than need. The communities served by Route 917 — Algona, Pacific — are lower-income, less politically visible, and less likely to generate advocacy pressure for improved stops, sidewalks, or service frequency. The result is that the riders most dependent on transit receive the least investment in the conditions that make transit safe and usable.

Design Implications

  • 01 — Equitable information infrastructure across all routes Route 917 stops display only a route number — no schedule, no destination, no real-time arrival data. Meanwhile, routes serving higher-income corridors in Seattle have full information kiosks at most stops. Deploying low-cost e-ink digital kiosks at underserved stops — with multilingual support — would directly address the information gap observed repeatedly during this study. E-ink displays are cost-effective, low-power, and weatherproof, making them feasible at scale across the Metro network.
  • 02 — Wheelchair capacity redesign on fixed-route buses Current Metro buses accommodate a fixed, limited number of wheelchair users per vehicle. When that capacity is reached, the next rider using a wheelchair is passed. On low-frequency routes running on hourly headways, a single missed bus can mean a missed connection and a failed trip entirely. Modular seating — interior configurations that allow standard seats to fold or be removed — would increase wheelchair capacity without requiring entirely new fleets. A phased retrofit program could prioritize routes with the lowest frequency and highest documented disability ridership.
  • 03 — Service frequency on community-dependent routes Hourly headways on routes serving transit-dependent communities create compounding vulnerability. A missed bus is not an inconvenience — it is a broken chain. Increasing service frequency on routes like 917, particularly during off-peak hours when vulnerable riders are most likely to travel to appointments, services, or community events, is the highest-impact policy change this study points toward.
  • 04 — Further research: equity mapping across the Metro network The contrast between information infrastructure on wealthy corridors versus Route 917 suggests a systematic pattern worth quantifying. A follow-up study mapping stop-level infrastructure quality against neighborhood income and demographic data across the full Metro network would provide the evidence base for an equity-driven capital investment argument to Metro leadership.

Reflection

What observation captured that interviews alone could not is the full architecture of a failed trip. An interview might ask a rider whether they felt the system was accessible — and they might say yes, or they might struggle to articulate the specific moment things went wrong. Observation documents the chain: the bus that passed, the hour wait, the missed connection. It captures frustration without requiring the rider to perform or recall it. It also surfaces experiences that riders may not have wanted to revisit in a formal interview setting — or simply would not have thought to mention without the right prompts.

A follow-up study would pair this observational foundation with structured methods to fill the gaps observation cannot reach. Interviews with Route 917 riders — particularly elderly residents and people with disabilities — would surface the experiences and trip decisions that could not be observed directly, including the trips people chose not to take because the system was not built for them. Extending observation to other routes serving similar demographics elsewhere in King County would allow for comparison and help distinguish local failures from systemic ones. Together, those methods would build the evidence base this study points toward but cannot complete alone.
